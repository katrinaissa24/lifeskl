Introduction to personal finance:

1. What is Personal Finance?
Personal finance is everything about how you manage your money in your life from how earning it, spending it, saving it, and investing it.
Think of it like a video game where your character has a "money bar" instead of a health bar. Every choice you make financially whether to buy something new, save for college, or spend on snacks affects your financial status or savings.
personal finance is responsible for things like:
- Budgeting: making a plan for how you'll spend your money.
- Saving: putting money aside for later needs.
- Investing: using your money to make more money, like buying stocks or starting a small business.
- Managing Debt: keeping track of what you owe and paying it off on time.
- Insurance: paying a company an amount of money regularly so they can help cover big unexpected costs.

2. Why Does It Matter?
Money isn't everything, but if you don't manage it well, you might:
- Run out of money before your next paycheck.
- Struggle to pay for emergencies (like your laptop breaking before exams).
- Miss out on future opportunities (like studying abroad or starting a business).
If you do manage it well, you can:
- Afford things without guilt.
- Save for cars, collage or travel. [typo: collage]
- Feel more in control of money instead of it controlling you.

Do you what to get that concert ticket? Be SMART with your money
- Specific: meaning be clear and detailed "I want to save $500 for a laptop" instead of "I want more money".
- Measurable: you can track progress, check how much you've saved so far
- Achievable: realistic for your situation if you earn $50 a week, don't aim for $1,000 in two weeks.
- Relevant: matches your priorities saving for a car makes sense if you need transportation.
- Time-bound: has a deadline.

Let's see how this would look like in real life: You got an entry level part-job eligible for students at Questk, let's say you get paid 200$ a month and you want to save 100$ for a school trip. Being skilled in personal finance will help you figure out how to save each week and where to cut spending and go on that trip!

"I know what's a budget why do I need one?"
It's not that simple, its actually way more beneficial than you think having a budget helps you pay your bills on time, save for emergencies, and stay in control of your money.

Key Reasons to Create a Budget
- To pay bills on time (avoid late fees or service cuts)
- To save for unexpected events (like medical bills or car trouble)
- To prepare for the future (e.g., college, big purchases)

Components of a Budget
- Income (all the money you earn or receive monthly)
- Expenses, broken into categories:
  - Fixed (rent, internet, subscriptions)
  - Variable (groceries, gas, entertainment)
- Savings helps build an emergency fund or pay for goals
- Difference between projected vs. actual spending (helps you adjust and improve)

Real-World Example
Sample monthly budget: Income: $3,000
Fixed costs (housing, utilities): $1,740
Entertainment: $50
Total expenses: $2,040
Leftover: $960 which goes into savings or other categories.

Tips on budgeting
- Tip: Record everything you spend for at least one week. Include small things like snacks or bus fare.
  Why: Even small purchases add up. If you don't track. [sentence cut off in original]
- Separate Needs vs. Wants
  - Needs: Essentials you must pay for rent, food, utilities, school supplies.
  - Wants: video games, movies, eating out.
  - Tip: Always cover needs first, then allocate money to wants if you can.
- Tip: Even if it's a small amount, put something aside each month. Start with 10% if you can.
  Why: Savings is your safety net and helps you reach bigger goals like a laptop, trip, or emergency fund.

Think of your money like energy points in a game:
- Income = energy bar refill
- Spending = using energy on actions
- Saving = storing energy for the boss fight (big goal or emergency)
Budgeting is just managing your energy wisely to win the month

## Game time!

### Game 1: The Income & Expense Challenge
Objective: Students must allocate their monthly income to cover essential expenses, savings, and fun, testing their understanding of budgeting basics.
Setup:
- Student is given an Income Card: $500 for the month.
- Expense Cards appear:
  - Rent $200 (Need)
  - Food $100 (Need)
  - Internet $40 (Need)
  - Snacks $30 (Want)
  - Movie Ticket $20 (Want)
- Goal Card: Save $50 for a concert. [original typo: "toal Card"]
Task: Allocate the $500 among Needs, Wants, and Savings.
Best Decision Path:
- Pay Needs first: Rent $200 + Food $100 + Internet $40 = $340
- Allocate Savings: $50 for concert goal
- Allocate remaining Wants: $30 for snacks + $20 for movie
Outcome: All bills covered, savings goal achieved, Happiness Heart full.
Real-Life Learning: Needs are top priority. Small amounts can be saved without sacrificing all fun. Budgeting helps reach goals efficiently.

### Game 2: The Surprise Life Card
Objective: Test students' adaptability to unexpected financial events.
Setup:
- Student starts with leftover money from Game 1: $60
- Draw 1 Surprise Card randomly:
  - "Bike repair: -$50"
  - "Found $20 on the street: +$20"
  - "Late subscription fee: -$15"
Task: Decide how to cover the cost or use the bonus without going negative.
Best Decision Path: Use part of leftover $60 to pay Bike repair $50, leaving $10 for entertainment.
Outcome: Needs and emergency handled, savings goal remains untouched, minor Happiness Heart drop.
Real-Life Learning: Life is unpredictable—always budget for surprises. Emergency funds prevent overspending or debt.

### Game 3: The Goal Tracker Quiz
Objective: Test students' understanding of prioritizing savings goals.
Setup: Student is asked: "You want to save $50 for a concert but only have $30 left after bills. What's the best decision?"
- Option A: Skip savings and buy snack
- Option B: Save $30 now and try to save the remaining $20 next month
- Option C: Borrow money from friends to reach $50
- Option D: Spend all on entertainment now
Best Decision Path: Option B
Outcome: Demonstrates patience and prioritization; avoids debt.
Real-Life Learning: Start small if you can't fully reach your goal. Consistent saving builds habits and achieves objectives over time.
