# Level 5 Lesson 1: Understanding Needs vs. Wants in Everyday Life

(Verbatim extraction from Notion. Note: "zoe ... his savings" pronoun inconsistency — use one pronoun.)

1. Why Spending Wisely Matters
Money is a tool. You can use it to cover your basic needs, enjoy life, or prepare for the future. The problem is: money is limited. You don't get unlimited cash, so every purchase is a choice.
Spending wisely doesn't mean never buying fun things. It means being intentional with your money so that you can afford what matters most to you.
Think of your money as a backpack. If you fill it with heavy rocks (unnecessary spending), you won't have space for water, food, and tools (the essentials). If you pack carefully, you'll survive the trip and still enjoy a few nice extras.

2. What Are Needs?
Needs = things that are essential for survival and basic daily life.
- Food and water → groceries, not fancy dining out.
- Housing → rent, utilities (electricity, water, internet).
- Transportation → bus card, gas, metro, or bike maintenance to get to school/work.
- Clothing → basic, weather-appropriate clothes (a winter coat in winter is a need; a designer jacket is a want).
- Healthcare → doctor visits, medicine, dental check-ups.
Example: Buying groceries = need. Ordering takeout three times a week = want.

3. What Are Wants?
Wants = things that are not essential but make life more enjoyable.
- Streaming subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify, Anghami)
- Gaming consoles, designer clothes, concert tickets, vacations
- The newest iPhone (when your current one works fine).
Wants aren't bad. They bring happiness and motivation. The problem comes when you spend too much on wants and don't have enough left for needs and savings.
Example: New school shoes because your old ones broke = need. Limited-edition designer because they "look cool" = want.

4. Why Do People Mix Them Up?
Companies spend billions on advertising to blur the line.
- Ads make you feel like you need the latest phone to "stay connected."
- Social media creates pressure (vacations, FOMO).
- Peer pressure (same sneakers as friends).
Rule of thumb: if you can live without it (and still be safe and healthy), it's a want.

5. The 50/30/20 Rule
- 50% → Needs (rent, food, transportation, healthcare, bills)
- 30% → Wants (shopping, hobbies, entertainment)
- 20% → Savings (emergencies, college, future goals)
Example: $600/month → $300 needs, $180 wants, $120 savings.

6. Real-Life Student Example
Zoe, a high school student, earns $200/month from tutoring.
- Needs: $80 (bus card, school lunch, phone bill)
- Wants: $60 (eating out, subscription, movie night)
- Savings: $60
At the end of the year, Zoe has $720 saved. Her friend, who spends everything on fast food and gaming, has $0. Who's better prepared for an emergency or opportunity? Clearly, Zoe.

7. How to Control Wants
- The 24-hour rule: If it's a want, wait 24 hours before buying. Often the urge fades.
- Track your spending: write down every purchase for a week.
- Budget for fun: give yourself a set "fun budget" each month.
- Save for big wants: instead of swiping a credit card, save gradually.

8. Lesson Takeaways
- Needs = survival and basic daily life. Wants = extras.
- Spending wisely = balance between needs, wants, savings.
- 50/30/20 organizes your budget simply.
- Small, disciplined habits make a huge difference over time.
