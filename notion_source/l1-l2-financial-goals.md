# Level 1 Lesson 2: Setting Financial Goals & Making Smart Decisions

(Verbatim extraction from Notion.)

Introduction
Think of money like a tool. If you don't know how to use a tool, you'll either break it or waste it. The same happens with money: if you don't set goals and plan, it slips away fast. This lesson is about how to create financial goals and make smart decisions that help you reach them.

Why Financial Goals Matter
Money doesn't just disappear it goes where you tell it to go. If you don't give it a "job," it usually ends up being spent on little things (snacks, rides, online shopping). Over time, that adds up and keeps you from reaching bigger goals.
Example: If you spend $5 on snacks every school day, that's about $100 a month. In a year, that's $1,200 enough for a laptop. (This shows how small habits can block bigger dreams.)

Types of Financial Goals
- Short-Term Goals (less than 1 year): These are things you want soon. Example: saving $200 for prom, $100 for sneakers, or $50 for a birthday gift. (They give quick rewards but require quick saving too.)
- Medium-Term Goals (1–5 years): Bigger things that take longer. Example: saving $1,000 for a laptop or $3,000 for a used car. (These require planning and discipline.)
- Long-Term Goals (5+ years): Dreams for the future. Example: saving for college tuition, a down payment on a house, or even retirement. (These goals shape your life, not just your week.)
Key idea: If you only focus on short-term goals (like sneakers or nights out), you'll struggle to reach long-term ones (like college).

The SMART Rule
Most people fail at goals because they're too vague. Saying "I want money" doesn't help. That's why strong goals follow the SMART method:
- Specific: Clear and detailed. ("I want to save for a phone.")
- Measurable: Has numbers. ("$600 total, $50 a month.")
- Achievable: Realistic. ("I can actually save $50 a month if I stop spending $20 on delivery.")
- Relevant: Important to you. ("I need this phone for school and personal use.")
- Time-bound: Has a deadline. ("In 12 months.")
Example:
❌ "I want to be rich someday." (Not SMART)
✅ "I will save $50 every month for one year to buy a $600 phone." (SMART)

Opportunity Cost (The Hidden Price of Choices)
Every choice has a cost, even if you don't see it right away. This is called opportunity cost what you give up when you choose one thing over another.
Example:
- If you buy sneakers today, you give up saving for concert tickets.
- If you spend $40 on takeout this week, you give up saving that $40 toward a laptop.
In real life: Smart people always ask, "If I buy this now, what am I losing later?"

Finding Balance
Life is about balance. If you only save, you'll feel miserable. If you only spend, you'll regret it later. The best approach is a mix:
- Cover your Needs first (food, transportation, school supplies).
- Save part of your money (even small amounts grow over time).
- Spend a little on Wants (fun things, entertainment) so you don't feel deprived.
Example: If you get $100:
- $50 goes toward savings (future goals).
- $30 for needs.
- $20 for fun.
This way, you enjoy today while still preparing for tomorrow.

How to Start Setting Goals Today
1. Pick one short-term goal (like saving $100 for sneakers).
2. Decide how much you'll save per week ($10 for 10 weeks).
3. Track your progress (use a notebook, an app, or even a jar).
4. Stick to the plan (don't spend the saved money early).
5. Celebrate when you reach it.
Once you succeed with a small goal, you can aim for bigger ones a laptop, a trip, or even a car.

## Game time!

### Mini-Game 1: Goal Sorter
Objective: Teach students to classify financial goals by time frame.
6 Goal Cards:
- "Buy new sneakers" ($80)
- "Save for prom dress/tux" ($150)
- "Save for a car" ($5,000)
- "Pay for college" ($20,000)
- "Go on vacation with friends" ($500)
- "Start retirement savings" ($100,000)
Task: Drag and drop each into Short-term, Medium-term, or Long-term.
Best: Short-term: Sneakers, Prom outfit. Medium-term: Vacation, Car. Long-term: College, Retirement savings.
Real-Life Lesson: Not all goals are the same. Big dreams take longer and require planning; smaller ones can be achieved quickly.

### Mini-Game 2: SMART or Not?
4 goal statements:
1. "I want to save money" → Not SMART (not specific)
2. "I will save $50 each month for 6 months to buy a new phone" → SMART
3. "I want to be rich someday" → Not SMART (too vague)
4. "I'll put away $20 a week for a year to buy a used car" → SMART
Real-Life Lesson: SMART goals make success more likely because they're clear and measurable.

### Mini-Game 3: The Choice Dilemma
Student has $100 left over after paying expenses. Options:
- A: Buy a pair of designer shoes ($100).
- B: Save for a concert in 2 months ($50 per month).
- C: Put it all into savings for college.
Best Decision: B or C depending on the goal.
Outcome: Shoes = no money left, no future benefit. Concert = experience + still affordable. College savings = bigger future benefit.
Real-Life Lesson: Every decision has an opportunity cost. Learning to weigh these choices makes smarter financial decisions.
