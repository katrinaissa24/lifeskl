# Level 4 Lesson 2: Balancing Risk & Reward, Your Investment Strategy

(Verbatim extraction from Notion.)

What is Risk vs. Reward?
Investing is all about balance. You want your money to grow, but higher growth usually comes with higher risk.
- Risk = chance you might lose money.
- Reward = potential gain from your investment.
Think of it like a skateboard ramp: the higher you go, the more exciting the ride (reward), but the bigger the fall if you lose balance (risk).
- Stocks can double your money but can also crash in value.
- Bonds grow slowly but are more stable.
Key Idea: Don't gamble everything on high-risk investments. Diversifying is the safest strategy.

How to Balance Risk & Reward

a) Diversification
Spread your money across different types of investments (stocks, bonds, mutual funds). One loss won't ruin your entire portfolio.
Example: $500 stocks, $300 bonds, $200 mutual fund. Stocks drop 20%, bonds rise 3%, fund rises 5% → total loss is minimized.

b) Age and Risk Tolerance
- Younger investors can usually take more risks (time to recover).
- Older investors should prioritize safer investments.
Example: 18-year-old: 70% stocks, 20% bonds, 10% mutual funds. 50-year-old: 30% stocks, 50% bonds, 20% mutual funds.

c) Setting Investment Goals
- Short-term goals (1–3 years): safer investments (bonds, savings accounts).
- Long-term goals (5+ years): can take more risk (stocks, mutual funds).
Example: Laptop next year → safer. College in 5 years → moderate. Retirement in 40 years → higher-risk OK.

Understanding Volatility
Volatility = how much an investment goes up and down in price.
Stocks are highly volatile; bonds are low-volatility.
High volatility can be scary short-term but can pay off long-term if managed wisely.
Tip: Don't check your portfolio every day — focus on long-term growth.

Building Your First Investment Strategy
1. Decide your risk tolerance.
2. Set clear goals (short vs long term).
3. Diversify across stocks, bonds, mutual funds.
4. Start small — even $5–$10 a week builds habits.
5. Review periodically and adjust.
Example: Student saving for college (5 years) and travel (10+ years). Plan: 40% bonds (safer for college), 30% mutual funds, 30% stocks (long-term travel growth).

Real-Life Tips
- Avoid "get rich quick" schemes.
- Always keep an emergency fund in savings before investing.
- Use simulated trading apps to practice without risk.
- Automate small, consistent investments — easier than timing the market.

Lesson Takeaways
- Risk vs. reward is the heart of investing.
- Diversify to protect your money.
- Match investments to your goals and timeline.
- Start early, invest consistently.
- Long-term patience beats short-term panic.

## Game time — Investor's Challenge (strategy simulation)

Part 1: Choose Your Risk Profile
- Conservative: 60% bonds, 20% mutual funds, 20% stocks. Safer, slower.
- Balanced: 40% stocks, 30% bonds, 30% mutual funds.
- Aggressive: 70% stocks, 20% mutual funds, 10% bonds.
✅ Best: depends on goals — beginners/short-term → conservative; long-term → balanced or aggressive.

Part 2: Set Your Goals
Short-term: laptop, summer camp, emergency fund. Long-term: tuition, car, travel, retirement.
✅ Short-term → safer (bonds/savings). Long-term → growth (stocks/funds).

Part 3: Monthly Market Events
- Tech Boom: stocks +20% → hold some, sell small portion.
- Market Dip: stocks –15% → stay invested if long-term.
- Interest Rate Increase: bonds +3%, stocks –5% → small rebalance OK.
- Unexpected Expense $200 → withdraw from safer assets, avoid selling risky assets at a loss.

Part 4: End of Year Feedback
- ≥ $1,200 Excellent: balanced risk + patience.
- $1,000–$1,199 Good: decent, could diversify better.
- < $1,000 Needs Improvement: too much risk or panicked.

Key lessons: risk must match goals; diversify; patience beats panic; emergency fund first; rebalance when events happen.
