# Level 6 Lesson 2: Staying Safe from Debt and Scams

(Verbatim extraction from Notion.)

Financial safety is not just about insurance and emergency funds. It's also about avoiding unnecessary debt and protecting yourself from scams. High school students are increasingly exposed to credit cards, online offers, and digital scams.

This lesson covers: how debt works and the risks; common scams and how to recognize them; strategies to avoid unnecessary debt; protecting personal/financial information; real-life examples.

1. Understanding Debt
Debt is money borrowed that must be paid back, usually with interest. Useful for major life events (college, car), dangerous if used carelessly.

Types of Debt:
- Credit Cards: buy now pay later; interest accumulates if you don't pay the full balance monthly; minimum payments can keep you in debt for years. Example: a $500 purchase with only minimum payments could end up costing over $700 in interest.
- Payday Loans: short-term, extremely high interest; trap borrowers in cycles. Example: borrow $200, pay back $250 in two weeks, then borrow again.
- Student Loans / Other Loans: can be necessary, must be managed carefully; no repayment plan = long-term burden.

Tips for Managing Debt:
- Only borrow what you can realistically pay back.
- Understand interest rates and repayment schedules BEFORE taking any loan.
- Track all balances and due dates.
- Avoid impulse borrowing for wants rather than needs.

2. Recognizing Common Scams
Scammers try to steal money or personal info with offers that seem urgent or too good to be true.
- Phishing Emails/Texts: pretend to be your bank; ask for login credentials, SSN, card info.
- Fake Job Offers: high pay for minimal work; require upfront payments for "training" or "processing fees."
- Lottery/Prize Scams: claim you won; ask for fees to claim it.
- Online Shopping/Marketplace Scams: fake products, no delivery guarantee.
Key Principle: If something seems too good to be true, it usually is. Verify through trusted sources before providing info or money.

3. Strategies to Avoid Debt
- Budgeting First: track income; list essential expenses; plan savings before non-essentials.
- Credit Card Caution: use only if you can pay the full balance each month; avoid impulse purchases.
- Avoid High-Interest Loans: borrow only when necessary, with a repayment plan; pay off ASAP.
- Emergency Fund: even $50–$100 prevents borrowing during unexpected events.
- Track and Review: check accounts/statements frequently; catch mistakes and fraud early.

4. Protecting Yourself from Scams
- Passwords: strong, unique per account.
- Online Behavior: don't share sensitive details online.
- Verification: contact banks/trusted sources directly if you receive suspicious messages.
- Physical Protection: shred important documents before disposal.
Real-life examples:
- A student got an email claiming they won $500. By calling the bank instead of clicking the link, they avoided losing their savings.
- Another borrowed $100 via payday loan; fees and interest quickly made it cost $180.

5. Planning Ahead
- Set clear goals for savings, spending, borrowing.
- Plan for major life events (college, moving out, car).
- Include protection measures (insurance, emergency fund, fraud awareness) in your budget.
- Review your financial plan regularly.

6. Lesson Takeaways
- Debt can be useful but dangerous if not managed carefully.
- Scams are common; good habits protect your money.
- Budgeting, emergency funds, responsible borrowing prevent financial stress.
- Checking accounts and staying cautious online safeguards your assets.

## Game time — Debt & Scam Survival Challenge

Part 1: Spot the Scam
Message claims you won $500 in an online contest, asks for bank info to claim the prize.
a) Click the link and enter bank info
b) Ignore the message and delete it ✓
c) Forward it to friends to see if they also got it
Why: Common scam. Banks never ask for sensitive info through email or text.

Part 2: Debt Dilemma
You want an $800 smartphone. You have $200 saved. Friend suggests a payday loan.
a) Take the payday loan, buy immediately
b) Wait and save gradually for the phone ✓
c) Use your credit card, pay only the minimum each month
Why: Payday loans = extremely high interest; minimum payments = long-term debt trap. Saving = patience + debt-free.

Part 3: Credit Card Choices
First credit card. Want $100 of clothes, need $50 for groceries.
a) Use the card for everything, pay the minimum next month
b) Buy groceries with the card and save cash for clothes ✓
c) Buy both with the card, worry about paying later
Why: Prioritize essentials, use credit responsibly, prevent debt accumulation.

Reflection: How do you recognize a scam before it's too late? What steps avoid unnecessary debt? How do budgeting and planning protect your money?
