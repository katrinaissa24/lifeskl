# Level 3 Lesson 2: Credit Scores & Why They Matter

(Verbatim extraction from Notion.)

What is a Credit Score?
A credit score is a number (usually between 300 and 850) that shows how trustworthy you are with money you borrow.
Think of it like your financial GPA. A high score = "good student" of money. A low score = lenders think you're risky.
Real-life comparison: Imagine you want to borrow your friend's car. If you always return it with a full tank, on time, and without dents, they'll lend it again. If you return it late, dirty, and empty, they'll stop trusting you. Banks think the same way.

Why Do Credit Scores Exist?
Banks, landlords, and even some employers don't know you personally. Instead, they look at your credit report and credit score which are like your financial reputation written down.
- Credit reports = history of what you did with credit (loans, cards, payments).
- Credit score = quick summary of that history, in number form.
A credit score is a shortcut for trust.

What Affects Your Credit Score? (The 5 Ingredients)
1) Payment History (35%) — Biggest factor. Did you pay bills on time? Even one missed payment can drop your score. Example: Forgetting to pay a $50 phone bill might hurt your score more than paying off a $500 loan late.
2) Amounts Owed (30%) — How much debt do you have right now? If your card limit is $1,000 but you've used $900, it looks like you're drowning in debt. Ideal rule: Use less than 30% of your available credit (on a $1,000 card, stay under $300).
3) Length of Credit History (15%) — How long have you been using credit? Don't close your oldest credit card — it helps your history look longer.
4) Credit Mix (10%) — A mix (card + car loan + student loan) shows you can handle different responsibilities.
5) New Credit (10%) — Applying for 5 new credit cards in 2 months looks desperate. That lowers your score.

Why Credit Scores Matter in Real Life
- Car Loans: Person A has 780 → 4% interest → pays $1,500 extra over the loan. Person B has 600 → 12% interest → pays $6,000 extra. Same car, same price, but Person B loses $4,500 more just because of their score.
- Renting: Landlords check your score to see if you'll pay rent on time. A bad score might get you rejected.
- Credit Cards: Good scores get perks (cash back, travel rewards, lower interest).
- Jobs: Some employers check your credit report (not the score itself) when hiring.

How to Build a Good Credit Score
1. Always pay on time. Even the minimum payment is better than late.
2. Don't max out cards. Keep usage below 30%.
3. Start small. A student card or secured credit card (deposit money first) builds history.
4. Don't close old accounts.
5. Check your credit report. Mistakes happen — you can fix errors for free.

Common Myths
- "I need debt to have a good score." Wrong. You need history, not debt. Use a card and pay it off monthly.
- "Checking my score lowers it." Wrong. Only lender hard inquiries when you apply can lower it. Checking it yourself doesn't.
- "Paying minimum payment is fine." Dangerous. It avoids late fees, but your balance still grows with interest.

The Big Picture
A credit score is your financial passport for loans, apartments, sometimes jobs. It can save you thousands if high. You control it with small, smart monthly choices. One mistake may stay on your report for years, but good habits slowly build trust.

## Game time! — Credit Score Adventure (branching/decision game)

Starting Score: 650 (average). Goal: reach 750+ (excellent).

Part 1: The Phone Bill — bill is $50 due on the 10th.
a) Pay on the 8th ✓ (+20 points)
b) Pay on the 15th (–40 points)
c) Ignore it until next month (–100 points)
Payment history is the #1 factor. Late = big penalty.

Part 2: The Credit Card Temptation — first card, $1,000 limit, want $200 sneakers.
a) Buy, pay full $200 when bill arrives ✓ (+30)
b) Buy, pay only $20 minimum (–20)
c) Max out the card with $1,000 of shopping (–70)
Paying in full builds history without interest. Maxing out looks risky (utilization).

Part 3: The Apartment Application — landlord checks credit, score 680.
a) You've paid on time, kept balances low ✓ (+50, approved!)
b) Missed payments, low score (denied, –30)
c) Applied for 5 new cards last month (looks desperate, –40)

Final Score Outcomes:
- 750+ (Excellent) → Banks, landlords, jobs trust you.
- 650–749 (Good/Average) → Doing okay, needs discipline.
- Below 650 (Poor) → Higher interest, fewer approvals.
