# Level 2 Lesson 2: Different Types of Savings Accounts & How Interest Works

(Verbatim extraction from Notion. Remove "(chat-gpt)" attribution and "the book" references in final content.)

Introduction: Where Should You Keep Your Money?
If you save money under your mattress or in a shoebox, it's safe from spending, but it doesn't grow. In fact, it loses value over time because of inflation (when prices rise, money buys less).
That's why we use savings accounts they keep your money safe and help it grow by earning interest (extra money the bank pays you for keeping money with them).

What is Interest?
Interest is basically a reward for saving.
- When you save, the bank pays you interest.
- When you borrow, you pay the bank interest.
Example: If you put $100 in a savings account with 2% annual interest, at the end of the year you have $102.

Types of Savings Accounts
a) Regular Savings Account
- Available at almost every bank. Easy to open and use.
- You can deposit and withdraw anytime.
- Pays a small amount of interest.
- Real life: Good for short-term savings like emergency funds or saving for a trip.

b) Certificates of Deposit (CDs)
- You agree to keep your money in the bank for a set time (like 6 months or 1 year).
- In return, you get a higher interest rate than a regular savings account.
- But: you can't touch the money until the time is up without paying a penalty (a fee for breaking the deal early).
- Real life: Good for money you don't need right away, like saving for next year's tuition.

c) Money Market Accounts
- Require a higher minimum balance.
- Pay higher interest than regular savings.
- Still allow access to your money when needed (with some limits).
- Real life: Good for larger savings — money you don't plan to spend soon but want available in emergencies.

d) Online Savings Accounts
- Offered by online banks (no physical branches).
- Usually pay higher interest rates because they have lower costs.
- Easy to manage through apps or websites.
- Real life: Great for tech-comfortable students who want to grow their money faster.

How Banks Protect Your Money
In the U.S., most bank accounts are insured by the FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation). If the bank fails, your money (up to $250,000) is still safe.
Real life: A shoebox or hiding spot doesn't protect your money. A bank account does.

Compound Interest: The Magic of Growth
Compound interest = when the interest you earn also starts earning interest.
Example:
- Year 1: You save $100 and earn 5% interest = $105.
- Year 2: You earn 5% on $105, not just $100 = $110.25.
Over many years, this "snowballs" into much bigger growth.
Real life: Compound interest is like planting a tree that keeps growing new branches. The longer you let it grow, the more powerful it becomes.

Choosing the Right Savings Option
- Emergency Fund / Short-term goals: Regular savings account or online savings.
- Medium goals (1–3 years): CDs (if you know you won't need the money).
- Large savings: Money market accounts (higher interest, still accessible).
- Long-term growth: Start with savings accounts, but eventually move toward investing.

Tips for High School Students
- Start with a regular savings account (easy and safe).
- Compare interest rates (online banks may give you more).
- Don't worry if you can only save small amounts — compound interest grows even tiny savings over time.
- Keep savings separate from checking/spending money, so you're not tempted to spend.
