Why This Matters
At some point, you'll want to buy something big a car, tuition, or even a home. You probably won't have all the money upfront. That's where credit (borrowing) comes in. But borrowing is never free, you pay back more than you borrowed because of interest (the fee for using someone else's money).

Key Concepts
- Credit Card: A card that lets you borrow money to a certain extent. If you don't pay full balance each month, you'll pay interest. Average credit card interest: specific% per year. [PLACEHOLDER ERROR]
- Loan: Money borrowed from a bank or lender. Types: Student loans. Car loans. Mortgages.
- Interest Rate (APR): The percentage you pay the lender for borrowing. Example: Borrow $1,000 at 10% APR = $100 in interest per year.
- Credit Score: A number that shows how trustworthy you are with borrowing. High score = better loan offers, lower interest. Low score = fewer options, higher costs.

Real-Life Example
Leila buys a $1,000 laptop with her credit card.
- If she pays it off in full that month → cost = $1,000.
- If she only pays the minimum payment ($30), the interest builds. She could end up paying over $1,300 total by the time it's paid off.
Borrowing isn't bad but if you don't understand interest, it can cost way more than you expect.
Takeaway: Credit is powerful, but it's never free. Always know what you'll owe before you borrow.

Debt Traps: How People Get Stuck
Debt becomes a problem when you borrow without a plan. Many people fall into traps like minimum payments or payday loans, and it keeps them in a cycle of owing money.

Common Traps
- Minimum Payments: Credit cards only ask you to pay a small % of your balance each month. Trap: You pay interest for years, making the item cost 2–3× more.
- Payday Loans: Short-term loans with extremely high interest (like 300–400% APR). Trap: You borrow $100 today, owe $120 in 2 weeks, and end up borrowing again.
- Impulse Buying on Credit: Buying things you want but don't need, thinking "I'll pay later."

Real-Life Example
Karim borrowed $500 using a payday loan for quick cash.
- The lender charged a $75 fee for 2 weeks.
- When he couldn't pay it back, he renewed it and was charged another $75.
- After 2 months, he owed $800 for a $500 loan.
What looked like a "helpful loan" became a trap.

## Game time!

### Scenario 1: The $1,000 laptop
You want to buy a $1,000 laptop for school. You don't have the money yet. Options:
a) Credit Card – Buy it now, but only pay $30/month minimum payment (interest = 20%).
b) Savings Plan – Wait 5 months, save $200/month, and buy it in cash.
c) Small Loan – Borrow from the bank with a 5% student loan, pay $50/month until it's done.
Best Decision: (c) or (b).
- Credit card minimum payment (a) → you'll end up paying over $1,300 for a $1,000 laptop.
- Small student loan (c) → very little extra.
- Savings (b) → no interest at all, but you wait longer.

### Scenario 2: The $200 emergency
You suddenly need $200 for car repairs. You have no savings. Choices:
a) Payday Loan: Borrow $200, owe $250 in 2 weeks.
b) Credit Card: Charge $200, pay it off fully next month.
c) Borrow from a Friend: Ask a trusted friend and pay them back.
Best Decision: (b) or (c).
- Payday loan (a) is a trap — if you can't pay back fast, fees pile up.
- Credit card (b) is okay if you pay the full $200 next month (no interest charged).
- Friend (c) can be safest — as long as you're responsible.
Lesson: Avoid high-interest loans — they keep you stuck.
